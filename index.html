LOG001: Council Trail
Nov. 8, 2025

· Temperature: 11–15°C
· Humidity: 72%
· Pine resin sweetness layered with moist earth, grass dew, and decaying leaves

Resin Light
A scent that opens with sharp cedar and cold air, the metallic bite of a November morning, then softens into damp moss, pine sap, and a trace of smoke. Beneath it lingers a quiet sweetness: decaying leaves, sunlight caught in bark.

Description
The forest at Council Trail smelled alive. Not the kind of "fresh" you read on candle labels, but something deeper, darker, with weight and age. It started the moment I stepped past the trailhead, where the air turned thick with cedar and wet bark. Each breath carried that sticky-green sharpness of pine resin, cut through by the faint salt of the Pacific. The scent clung to the back of my throat, sweet and medicinal at once — a smell that makes you forget the seven-million-dollar mansion sitting right in front of the trailhead because no amount of glass and marble can outshine the smell of old trees doing what they've done for centuries, simply existing.

The sun was kind this morning. It warmed the edges of damp leaves and drew sweetness from the sap. When my sleeve brushed a low branch, its oil marked me. Suddenly the scent followed me, softening over time into something deeper, smokier, like the ghost of a campfire on forest skin. Two hours later, when I reached the end of the trail, I could still smell it on my hands — faint but certain. Proof that scent can hold a memory longer than words.

If this morning were a fragrance, it would smell of stillness. Of motion remembered. Of thought made clear in solitude, the calm that follows movement.

LOG002: Elk Mountain Trail, BC, Canada
Nov. 18, 2025

· Temperature: 11–15°C
· Humidity: 72%
· Papery, sun-softened leaves drifting into mineral air and clean, transparent snow; resin warming into something honey-soft.

Frostlight
A scent that carries the warmth of sun-softened leaves, the crisp clarity of first snow, and the earthy sweetness of grass after a fresh rain.

Description
The drive up to the entrance of Elk Mountain wasn't winter yet, just cold, the kind of metallic clarity that lands on skin before the weather decides what it wants to be. The trail began in late-autumn quiet, leaves soft and papery underfoot, their sweetness fading into the damp air.

Snow didn't arrive until halfway up. First as a few uncertain flakes, drifting more like ideas than weather. Then the air thickened. The forest dimmed. Pine needles snapped sharply under the growing frost, releasing a green, resinous breath into the cold.

By the time we reached the summit, the season had shifted its weight entirely. Snow lay heavy on the branches, smoothing every edge of the mountain into white. The scent of the world changed with it, mineral, clean, transparent, resting atop the darker warmth of earth preparing to sleep.

A threshold crossed.
A season turning in real time.
A fragrance shaped by the transition of autumn's fading warmth into winter's quiet clarity.
